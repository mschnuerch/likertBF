Welcome!

With this Shiny app, you can calculate Bayes factors for two distributions on some ordinal (e.g., Likert-type) items. To analyze your data, you must enter the observed category frequencies for each condition in the first step. The frequencies must be separated by a , (you can also use ; or /). You can plot the data by clicking on the respective button. This will create an ROC-type plot and a difference plot for the cumulative proportions in the two conditions. Optionally, you can provide category labels which will appear in the plot.

The data are modeled as a latent normal distribution where response categories correspond to ordered regions on the latent scale (as in SDT). Response probabilities correspond to the areas of the latent regions under the curve, and the parameters of interest are the bounds of these regions in each condition. Each bound in each condition is modeled as some grand mean (alpha) and a specific difference between conditions (theta).

We specify four statistical models:

  • Unconstrained Model: No constraints on theta (there is an effect of condition but it's not captured by one relation)
  • Dominance Model: All thetas are positive/negative (there is an effect in one direction)
  • Shift Model: There is one theta for all bounds (there is a constant effect)
  • Null Model: Theta is 0 (there is no difference between conditions)

To compare these models by means of Bayes factors, you need to specify priors on the alphas and thetas. These are centered normal distributions with standard deviation balpha and btheta. Define these variances to express your prior beliefs. Note that you must carefully choose the prior settings for the case at hand. Your choice will affect your conclusions (as it should).

You can also specify the settings of the MCMC sampling algorithm. The number of posterior samples must be sufficiently high (e.g., 200,000). By default, the variances of the proposal distributions will be tuned before the sampling process to achieve an acceptance rate of about 30%. Optionally, you can disable auto-tuning and specify these variances yourself. You can check convergence of the MCMC algorithm by plotting the chains from the unconstrained model.
